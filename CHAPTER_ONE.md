// A grim, unforgiving dark fantasy setting where civilization is nothing but a thin coat of paint over endless rot.

## **THE CELLAR**

The scratching had stopped an hour ago.

That was worse than the sound.

Eldrin sat with his back against the mill's stone wall, bow across his knees, listening to the silence press down like a physical weight. The tiny flame at Malrik's staff tip cast long, trembling shadows that seemed to reach for them with patient, hungry fingers. Outside, the dark was absolute. Inside, it was only slightly less so.

"They're testing us," Garrick said quietly. "Probing for weakness."

"How long until dawn?" Vespatian's voice was hoarse. His mace rested against his shoulder, and his robes were torn at the collar where something had nearly gotten a grip on him during the last assault. The black veins on his forearms had climbed higher. Eldrin could see them even in the dim light—thin, dark lines threading up toward his elbows like cracks in old plaster.

"Three hours," Eldrin said. He'd been counting. "Maybe four."

"We won't last four hours," Malrik said flatly. She was still slumped against the far wall, her wrapped hands pressed flat against the cold stone. The flame at her staff tip flickered, guttered, steadied. "The door won't hold another serious push. The wood's rotting. The bolt's already cracked."

As if in answer, something **slammed** against the door.

Not a scratch. Not a test. A **blow**, heavy and deliberate, that made the entire frame shudder. Dust rained down from the ceiling. The bolt held, but the wood around it splintered with a sound like breaking teeth.

"Fuck," Garrick breathed.

The second blow came before they could move. The door buckled inward, the grinding stone they'd wedged against it sliding backward with a screech of stone on stone. The timber beam Vespatian had jammed under the handle cracked, one end lifting off the ground.

"The door won't—" Malrik started.

"I know," Eldrin said. He was already moving, scanning the mill's interior with the practiced eye of a man who'd learned to find exits in the dark. The upper floor was a maze of rotting beams and holes. The windows were too small, too high. The walls were solid stone, four feet thick at least.

The third blow came with a sound like the world breaking.

The door exploded inward. The grinding stone tumbled across the floor like a child's toy. The timber beam snapped in half, its pieces clattering into the darkness. For one terrible moment, the threshold was open, and the dark beyond it was **absolute**.

Then something pale drifted through.

Not a shambler. Not one of the lesser dead. A **vampire**, its form shifting like smoke, its eyes burning like coals. It paused in the doorway, taking in the four of them with an expression of mild amusement. Behind it, the grass rustled with movement. More shapes. More hunger.

"Back," Eldrin hissed. "Move."

Garrick was already moving, his rapier coming up in a perfect guard. Vespatian raised his mace, his lips moving in a prayer that sounded more like a curse. Malrik's staff flared, the flame at its tip burning brighter, casting the vampire's pale features into sharp relief.

The creature smiled.

It took a step forward, and Malrik threw fire.

The spell erupted from her staff like a living thing, a torrent of white-hot flame that should have melted armor to slag. The vampire walked through it without breaking stride. Its pale skin didn't even **blister**. It just kept coming, drifting through the inferno like a man walking through light rain.

"Go!" Eldrin shouted. He loosed an arrow point-blank, aiming for the creature's eye. The shaft buried itself to the fletching, and the vampire didn't even **flinch**. It just reached up, casually snapped the arrow, and kept walking.

Garrick met it with his rapier, a perfect thrust that should have driven the blade through its sternum. The creature caught the blade between two fingers and **twisted**. The rapier snapped like kindling. Garrick stumbled backward, staring at the broken hilt in his hand.

"The floor!" Eldrin shouted. "There—"

He'd seen it in the firelight, a faint seam in the stone near the mill's center. An old hatch, its edges worn smooth by centuries of use. He was already running, his bow abandoned, his hands reaching for the iron ring set into the stone.

Vespatian was right behind him, his mace forgotten. Malrik threw another spell, and another, each one burning hotter than the last, each one **accomplishing nothing**. The vampire drifted through the flames like they were **nothing**, and behind it, the shambling dead were pouring through the broken door.

Eldrin's fingers found the ring. He **pulled**.

The hatch was heavy, its hinges rusted, but it came up with a screech of protesting metal. Below, a ladder descended into darkness. The smell that rose from the cellar was old—dust and stone and something faintly sweet and **wrong**.

"Down!" he shouted.

Garrick went first, his broken rapier still in his hand. Vespatian followed, his robes tangling around his legs as he descended. Malrik was last, her staff held high, the flame casting wild shadows on the cellar walls as she climbed down.

The vampire reached the hatch just as Eldrin pulled it shut.

He didn't have time to lock it. Didn't have time to do anything but **slam** the iron ring down and throw the bolt home. The creature's pale hand shot through the gap, grasping, and Eldrin **slammed** the hatch down on its wrist.

The hand didn't scream. It didn't bleed. It just **pulled**, and the hatch buckled upward, the bolt cracking under the strain.

"Help me!" Eldrin gasped.

Garrick was there, throwing his weight against the hatch. Vespatian added his strength. The hand pulled harder, and for one terrible moment, Eldrin thought the bolt would **snap**.

Then Malrik raised her staff and **burned**.

The flame erupted from the hatch's edges, white-hot and furious. The hand **released**, and the hatch slammed shut with a **bang** that echoed through the cellar. Above, something **screamed**—a sound like ice cracking, like the world breaking, like something that had never known pain suddenly **learning**.

The scratching started immediately. Frantic now. Desperate. The hatch buckled and heaved as something threw itself against it again and again, but the bolt held. The stone held. The darkness below held.

For now.

---

They stood in the cellar, breathing hard, listening to the assault above. The hatch was solid, but it wouldn't hold forever. Already, cracks were forming in the stone around the bolt. Already, dust was raining down like snow.

Malrik's staff was the only light. The flame at its tip was burning low, guttering like a candle in a dying wind. In its glow, Eldrin could see the cellar clearly: a long, rectangular chamber carved from living stone, its walls lined with empty shelves. Tools, once. Grain stores. The detritus of a working mill.

And at the far end, a **breach**.

The wall had been broken long ago, a jagged hole perhaps six feet across, its edges worn smooth by time. Beyond it, darkness. Beyond that, a tunnel, its mouth yawning like the throat of something vast and hungry.

"No," Vespatian said quietly. "Absolutely not."

"We don't have a choice," Eldrin said. He was already moving toward the tunnel, his eyes adjusting to the deeper dark. "The hatch won't hold. We need to move."

"Into the dark?" Malrik's voice was tight, controlled, but Eldrin could hear the **fear** underneath. "Into a tunnel that could lead anywhere?"

"It leads away from them," Garrick said. He was examining the breach, running his fingers along the stone. "That's enough."

Above, the hatch buckled again. A crack appeared in the stone, thin as a hair, and dust poured through like sand through an hourglass.

"Now," Eldrin said.

Malrik went first, his staff held high, the flame casting wild shadows on the tunnel walls. The passage was narrow, barely wide enough for a man to walk upright, and it descended at a gentle angle into the earth. The air was cold and still, and it smelled of **old things**. Old stone. Old water. Old **death**.

Vespatian followed, his mace held ready. Garrick came next, his broken rapier still in his hand. Eldrin brought up the rear, listening to the assault above fade into the distance as they descended deeper into the dark.

Behind them, the hatch finally **gave way**.

The sound echoed down the tunnel like thunder, and for a moment, Eldrin thought he heard **laughter**. Not human laughter. Something older. Something that had been waiting in the dark for a **very long time**.

He didn't look back. He just kept walking, deeper into the tunnel, deeper into the dark, following the faint glow of Malrik's staff into the **unknown**.

The mill was behind them now. The light was behind them. The world above was behind them.

Below, in the darkness, something **stirred**.

And it was **hungry**.

## **THE DESCENT**

The tunnel twisted deeper into the earth, its walls becoming smoother, more deliberately carved. This was not a natural passage. This was **made**. Built by hands, shaped by purpose, abandoned by time.

"How far down do you think we are?" Vespatian's voice echoed strangely in the confined space.

"Far enough," Eldrin said. He was counting steps, a habit from his ranger days. Fifty paces. A hundred. The tunnel showed no sign of ending.

The walls began to change. The rough stone gave way to something smoother, almost polished. And there were **marks** on the walls—scratches, symbols, things that might have been writing in a language none of them recognized.

"This is old," Malrik said quietly. "Older than the mill. Older than the settlement."

"Older than the empire?" Garrick asked.

"Maybe," Eldrin said. He'd seen ruins before, in the deep forests of Mishark. Places where the old world had left its **fingerprints**. This felt like that. Like walking through the bones of something vast and **long dead**.

The tunnel opened into a chamber.

It was vast, its ceiling lost in shadow even with Malrik's flame. The floor was smooth stone, worn by countless footsteps. And there, in the center of the chamber, was a **well**.

Not a well for water. A well for **something else**. Its mouth was perfectly circular, perhaps ten feet across, and it descended into darkness so complete that even Malrik's flame couldn't penetrate it. The air rising from it was cold and carried a smell like **old blood and older magic**.

"We're not going down there," Vespatian said flatly.

"We're not," Eldrin agreed. He was scanning the chamber, looking for other exits. There—a passage on the far side, leading deeper into the earth. And another, to the left, ascending slightly. "We keep moving."

But Garrick had stopped. He was staring at the well, his expression unreadable.

"Garrick," Eldrin said. "Move."

"There's something down there," Garrick said quietly. "I can feel it. Like... like something's watching."

"Everything's watching," Malrik said. Her voice was tight, controlled. "That's the problem. We need to move."

They chose the ascending passage. It was narrower than the first tunnel, and it twisted upward in a spiral that made Eldrin's sense of direction spin. But it was moving **away** from the well, and that was enough.

The passage opened into another chamber, this one smaller, its walls lined with **alcoves**. In each alcove, something lay. Bones, mostly. Arranged carefully, deliberately. Some were human. Some were **not**.

"Burial chamber," Vespatian whispered. "This is a tomb."

"We're in a tomb," Malrik said flatly. "We're walking deeper into it."

"Do you want to go back?" Eldrin asked.

Malrik didn't answer.

They pressed on, moving through the darkness, following the passages as they twisted and turned through the earth. The air grew warmer as they descended, and the smell changed—less like death, more like **stone and time**.

And then, finally, they saw **light**.

Not the warm light of Malrik's flame. Real light. Pale and gray, filtering down from somewhere above. Daylight.

"The sun," Vespatian breathed. "We made it."

The passage opened into a larger chamber, and there, in the ceiling, was a **hole**. Not large—perhaps three feet across—but it was open to the sky. And through it, the first hints of **dawn** were beginning to show.

Eldrin looked at the others. Garrick's face was streaked with blood and dust. Vespatian's robes were torn, his mace dented from impacts that should have killed him. Malrik's hands were shaking, her staff's flame burning low.

They were alive. Barely. But they were alive.

"We made it," Eldrin said quietly.

But as he looked around the chamber, he saw something that made his blood run cold. The walls were covered in **symbols**. Not random scratches. Deliberate markings. And in the center of the chamber, carved into the stone floor, was a **circle**. A binding circle, if Eldrin's knowledge of the old texts was correct.

And in the center of the circle, something was **carved into the stone itself**. A shape. A form. Something that had been **bound** here, long ago.

Something that was **no longer bound**.

"We need to leave," Eldrin said quietly. "Now."

"What is it?" Vespatian asked.

"I don't know," Eldrin said. "But I know we don't want to find out."

They climbed toward the light, toward the hole in the ceiling, toward the dawn that was breaking above them. Behind them, in the darkness of the chamber, something **stirred**.

And in the binding circle, the stone where something had been carved began to **crack**.
